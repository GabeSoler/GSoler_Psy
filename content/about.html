My life story...

I am from Chile, a place many people in Europe seem to feel exotic. I have the rare condition of inheriting two european passports, Spanish and Italian, from my two grandmothers. In the context of the Chilean dictatorship these passports where a of high value and they where used by some of my family members.

Being multinational has been part of my life, but at the same time, it was a far away thing as I was growing up. I always felt I was not going to travel away from Chile, until I decided to move to Scotland to pursue further studies

Life in Chile was not easy, and the things we lived as normal are sometimes shocking for people at this side of the world. Having a extremely violent dictatorship the first seven years of my life left a mark in me and I could see that mark in the people around me

Fear was a comon emotion, fear of talking with others too much, fear of staying out until late, fear of the police officers, fear of walking alone

Some therapist work with trauma as if it is something irrational, I sadly desagree. When you have lived traumatic experiences it is a reality that goes inside your bones, and it is not that easy to work it out

I was a curious and explorative child, from early age a bit more conerned with ideas, art and spirituality than the 'worldly' things. I was prone to ask difficult cuestions in my catholic primary like 'what is the sex of God?', or 'when monkies will evolve as humans?'

My curiosity for other people showed also at early age, to the point that a neighbour would prefer to walk a bit longer than answer my questions when he was arriving home. That gave me the nick name of 'the why' in my streets, as I would ask eveyone details of why they where doing whatever they where doing

Having a nickname and knowing my curiosity was annoying made me stop asking too many questions to people, but at that time I was learning to read, so books where my new source of exploration

Around eight I made my first acrilic painting, which my grandmother stores as a little treasure. I was fascinated by colour from early age and painting and drawing where a safe space for me. At school I would draw to relax and to avoid talking to people and get called off

Why telling this things about me? Because these are some of the traits I bring with me. A relationship with politics and systemic trauma, curiosity about people why people do what they do, and a intense interest for arts and creativity

Those traits have been there from early childhood, but I have not always loved my own particular way of being in the world. Quite early I understood what bullying meant and how much it hursts to be dicriminated.

Even now sometimes I struggle showing these traits to people, because in a 'macho' culture being curious and sensitive where not appreciated, at least not as a good thing

I felt different from so early that I lernt to hide, and to mask. I started wondering how to be like the other kids and tried to be someone else

Nowadays we have a name for this things: Neurodiversity. I prefer to not put myself in one category, but I know my traits have been slighly different from forever: school was easy intelectually but so hard socialy

I felt lonely inside until I found a friedn with whom I could talk abstractly, and slowly I started to find people who also where sensitive and more abstract

Stydiyng psychology was such a treat for me, as I had the chance to learn all the different approaches and ideas about the human mind, however things did not get too easy as I had a mental health crisis mid way through

How could I have been a therapist if all the trauma acumulated from childhood would not have popped out? I propably would have been an academic actually.

I think that having a crisis and needing to go through different specialist showed me so much about being fragile, suffering, the limitations of the profession and also its wonders. There was so much I did not even imagine before, and so much to do.

After coming back and finishing psychology I continued studying and seeing clients, as some sort of indivisible path that lead to my PhD in Edinburgh.

Here I was able to develop my intuitions and ideas in a way I never could before. Having two scholarships that covered my fees and mantainanace I had time to study, do art, explore as much as I wanted.

And that lead me to new insights and theories about what it means to be human. I realised we are creative beings who live between two realms, the inner world of intensities, arts and dreams and the world of objects, machines and clear measurments.

This space between I call Transitional, the space of play, culture, imagination and the creation of professions and arts. It is from a place of understanging our fragility and our beauty that making therapy makes sense, a dance between madness and beauty, pain and growth
